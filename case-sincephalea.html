Turning a heavy migraine tracker into an insight-driven therapeutic tool

sinCephalea is a prescription-grade digital health app for migraine patients that combines CGM sensor data, nutrition logging and migraine journaling. In this self-initiated UX project, I took an end-to-end look at the product and redesigned the core experience: from “just tracking” to explaining patterns between migraine, glucose and daily behaviour.

The problem

Patients log every meal and migraine episode for weeks, but mainly see raw charts and dense, bright screens. The effort–to–insight ratio is poor, and the UI is hard to use during attacks.

What I did

I ran a diary study, heuristic evaluation and competitive benchmark, then reframed the focus from data entry to insight delivery. I designed a migraine-friendly dark UI, quick episode and medication flows with follow-ups on effectiveness, and an explainable insight layer that turns patterns into short, empathetic messages.

Why it matters

The concept increases perceived value (“What does this mean for my migraine?”), creates richer longitudinal data for patients and clinicians, and fits the real context of use: pain, light sensitivity and high tracking burden.

1. Insightful, explainable patterns instead of raw data

I designed a dedicated insights view that combines CGM data, meals and migraine episodes, and uses an AI-supported layer to turn them into clear, explainable patterns instead of raw charts.

  • Narrative summaries such as “4 of 6 recent attacks followed a steep glucose rise within 2–4 hours”
  • “Why am I seeing this?” explanations that link each hint to concrete data points
  • Configurable frequency of insights and a calm, non-judgmental tone of voice

Calendar overview surfaces recurring patterns across weeks, e.g. clustering of attacks with unstable glucose days.

2. Log fast in pain, add detail later

I redesigned the migraine flow so patients can log an episode with one tap during pain and fill in details, medication and effectiveness later.

3. A migraine-friendly, low-stimulus interface

I created a dark, low-stimulus UI that is actually usable during migraine attacks: calm contrasts, clear hierarchy and reduced motion.

  • Dark-mode UI with muted accent colours and WCAG-compliant contrast
  • Larger tap targets and bottom-aligned actions for easier use in pain

Outcome & learnings

Expected product impact

The redesign shifts sinCephalea from fragmented logging screens to a coherent insight layer, so patients see understandable migraine patterns instead of raw charts.

Integrated episode, meal and medication flows, plus the calendar and insight views, create richer, more structured longitudinal data that can support more meaningful self-management and doctor–patient conversations.

What this project shows about me

I can take a complex, data-heavy health product, spot where effort and value are misaligned, and redesign the experience so that data, AI and UI work together as a calm, explainable companion for people in a vulnerable context.
